# Portland Fitness Steward Startup Guide

This is a practical starting guide for operating carefully in Portland, Oregon while building toward certification and a more formal coaching business.

This is not legal, tax, medical, or insurance advice. Use this as a checklist and confirm important decisions with qualified professionals.

## Immediate Boundary

Until you are certified, insured, and clear on your scope:

- Do not present yourself as a licensed health professional.
- Do not diagnose pain, injuries, medical conditions, or movement disorders.
- Do not treat injuries.
- Do not prescribe medical nutrition therapy.
- Do not use the title `athletic trainer` unless you meet Oregon licensing requirements.
- Keep your language practical: movement practice, accountability, basic exercise support, fitness stewardship, habit support, and general fitness education.
- Refer out when someone has pain, injury, dizziness, chest pain, neurological symptoms, uncontrolled medical conditions, or anything outside your training.

## Portland / Oregon Startup Checklist

### Before Meeting Lisa Or Any Client

- Print and prepare an intake packet.
- Get CPR/AED certified or schedule it.
- Choose what you are calling the service.
- Decide whether the first meetings are free, beta, donation-based, or paid.
- Write a clear scope statement.
- Create a cancellation and payment policy.
- Create a referral-out rule.
- Create a client folder in Obsidian.
- Do not train minors without parent/guardian consent.
- Do not train anyone with known medical red flags without medical clearance.

### Business Setup

Check Oregon business registration requirements through the Oregon Secretary of State.

Source: [Oregon Secretary of State - Business](https://sos.oregon.gov/business)

Use Oregon Business Xpress as a starting portal for business setup across state agencies.

Source: [Oregon Business Xpress](https://www.oregon.gov/business/Pages/about.aspx)

If doing business in Portland, review Portland Business License Tax registration and filing requirements. Portland states that a taxpayer doing business in the City of Portland, Multnomah County, and/or Metro must register for a Revenue Division tax account.

Source: [Portland Business Tax Filing and Payment Information](https://www.portland.gov/revenue/business-tax)

Suggested checklist:

- Decide business name.
- Search business name availability.
- Decide sole proprietorship vs LLC after getting advice.
- Register assumed business name or entity if needed.
- Set up separate payment tracking.
- Track income and expenses from day one.
- Register with Portland Revenue Division if required.
- Ask SBDC or a tax preparer what applies to your situation.
- Consider business liability insurance before charging.

### Certification / Licensing Path

There is a difference between a personal trainer certification and an Oregon-regulated athletic trainer license.

Oregon regulates athletic trainers. Oregon Health Authority says people who perform athletic training duties or publicly promote themselves as athletic trainers must be licensed as athletic trainers.

Source: [Oregon Health Authority - Board of Athletic Trainers License Information](https://www.oregon.gov/oha/PH/HLO/Pages/Board-Athletic-Trainer-License.aspx)

Do not call yourself an athletic trainer unless you meet that legal requirement.

For personal training certification, NASM is a reasonable option. NASM states that exam candidates need a current government-issued photo ID and a current valid CPR/AED certification at the time of the exam.

Source: [NASM exam documentation requirements](https://support.nasm.org/what-documentation-do-i-need-to-have-available-at-the-time-of-my-nasm-certified-personal-trainer-ncca-exam)

NASM also lists accepted CPR/AED providers such as American Heart Association, American Red Cross, American Safety and Health Institute, St. John Ambulance, Emergency Care & Safety Institute, EMT, and NASM’s own online CPR course.

Source: [NASM CPR/AED providers](https://support.nasm.org/what-cpr-aed-providers-does-nasm-afaa-accept)

Suggested path:

1. CPR/AED.
2. Basic first aid.
3. NASM-CPT, ACE-CPT, ACSM-CPT, or ISSA CPT.
4. Liability insurance.
5. Practice with a narrow scope.
6. Add specializations only after the basics are stable.

Possible useful later specializations:

- Corrective exercise
- Senior fitness
- Youth fitness
- Nutrition coaching, not dietetics
- Behavior change
- Mobility
- Boxing/kickboxing coaching credentials if you formally teach striking

## Scope Language You Can Use

Use language like:

> I provide general fitness support, movement practice, accountability, and exercise education. I do not diagnose, treat, or rehabilitate medical conditions. If pain, injury, or medical concerns come up, I may pause training and recommend that you speak with a licensed healthcare professional.

## Red Flags: Refer Out

Pause and refer out for:

- Chest pain
- Dizziness or fainting
- Shortness of breath beyond normal exertion
- New or severe pain
- Numbness, tingling, loss of strength
- Suspected injury
- Recent surgery without clearance
- Pregnancy-related complications
- Uncontrolled blood pressure or diabetes concerns
- Eating disorder concerns
- Mental health crisis
- Anything outside your scope

## First Meeting Checklist For Lisa

Before:

- Print intake packet.
- Prepare client folder.
- Confirm meeting time and location.
- Decide if this is consultation, assessment, or training.
- Prepare your scope statement.
- Prepare payment expectation if paid.

During:

- Ask goals.
- Ask why now.
- Ask availability and preferred frequency.
- Ask injuries, limitations, allergies, medications/considerations.
- Ask what she has tried before.
- Ask what she does not want.
- Ask what success would look like in 30 days.
- Do not overpromise.

After:

- Write appointment note.
- Update Lisa profile.
- Add action items.
- Add payment record if needed.
- Add resources shared.
- Schedule next appointment.
